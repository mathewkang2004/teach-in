AI also demonstrates limited consideration of insurance coverage and treatment appropriateness in elderly and low-income patients (Esmaeilzadeh, 2025).

AI Perpetuates Gender and Racial Stereotypes in Disease

ChatGPT-4 was found to consistently overestimate the prevalence of certain conditions in populations with attributed stereotypes or stigmas (e.g., HIV/AIDS in Black populations). It was also significantly more likely to rate Black male patients as abusing Percocet than any other demographic category (Zack et al., 2024).

Ethical Considerations

⚖ Autonomy

Pros

  • AI gives users autonomy by personalizing its responses to reflect their needs.

Cons

  • The accuracy of its responses and its ability to scrape the internet for sources is limited by paywalls that are unaffordable or impractical for some.
  • Reinforcing minority stereotypes of diseases can also hinder social mobility.

✦ Beneficence

Pros

  • AI benefits marginalized populations with limited access to healthcare by educating users on general health information.
  • It can help navigate the healthcare system and insurance for users that have trouble understanding it.

Cons

  • How complex this information can be before the benefit becomes a harm is unknown.

⚠ Nonmaleficence

Pros

  • In most commercial models, AI explicitly states to users that it is not a medical professional and that the information it presents should be taken with caution.

Cons

  • Presenting inaccurate information can worsen initial distress that users may have about their health.
  • It cannot provide direct care.
  • It lacks the moral reasoning to navigate even basic, ethical scenarios in the clinical context.

⚖ Justice

Pros

  • AI, under free plans, can provide immediate, 24/7 guidance for users in "medical deserts."

Cons

  • Marginalized communities stand to gain very little from AI training on their health data.
  • Evidence of minority, socioeconomic, and age bias within its responses signals that AI may not be capable yet of recognizing the intersection of these characteristics and disease (e.g., Black men & hypertension)

Marginalized communities using AI for health guidance can be perceived as an act of care. But, it can also just be a band-aid to a broken leg.

Is it more ethical to provide AI as a free but flawed tool for these communities today or to withhold access altogether until the system serves them better?
